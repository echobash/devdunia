Subnetting — why the sender checks "same network or not?"

/24
A device ANDs its own IP with the subnet mask, then ANDs the destination IP with the same mask. Both results match → destination is local. They differ → send to the default gateway. This one bitwise check is the fork in the road for every packet.

Networking zero to hero — the concepts behind the diagram

🌐 What is a network?

Just devices connected so they can exchange data. Data never travels as one big blob — it's chopped into small chunks called packets, each carrying addressing info so the network knows where to deliver it.

Like sending a book through the post one page at a time, each page in its own addressed envelope.

📮 IP address

A device's postal address, e.g. 192.168.1.10. It's logical: it tells which neighborhood (network) and which house (host) a device is, and it changes when the device moves to another network.

Move to a new city → you get a new postal address.

🏷️ MAC address

A permanent serial number burned into every network card at the factory, e.g. 3C:7A:8B:11:00:0A. It never changes and only reaches the next directly-connected device — it can't find anything across the world.

Your name vs your address: the address (IP) gets the letter to the right house, the name (MAC) says who inside picks it up.

🔀 Switch

Connects devices inside one network. It only reads MAC addresses and keeps a MAC table: "PC-A is on port 1, PC-B on port 2" — then delivers each frame out exactly one port. It never looks at IP addresses.

A mailroom clerk inside one building: knows every desk, never leaves the building.

📦 Router

Connects different networks — every one of its ports sits in a different subnet. It reads the destination IP, checks its routing table (a map of which networks lie in which direction), and forwards the packet one network closer. Your home "WiFi router" does exactly this.

A post office: reads the city on the envelope, picks the right road.

🏘️ Subnet — and why we split networks

A neighborhood of IP addresses, e.g. 192.168.1.0/24 = everything starting 192.168.1. We split because: 1) broadcasts ("who has this IP?!") are heard by everyone on a subnet — a million devices on one network would drown in shouting; 2) organization — like postcodes, they make delivery decisions fast; 3) security — office and guest WiFi can be walled off.

A city split into neighborhoods instead of one endless street.

🚪 Default gateway

The router's address on your own subnet — the exit door. The rule every device follows: destination on my subnet → deliver directly; anywhere else → hand to the gateway and let the router worry.

The one door out of your neighborhood; you don't need a world map, just the way to the door.

✉️ Packet vs frame

The packet (IP layer) is the letter — it carries the final destination and survives the whole journey unchanged. The frame (Ethernet layer) is the delivery van carrying it one hop — a fresh van, with fresh MACs, on every link.

Same letter, three different vans. The packet strip above proves it.

📢 ARP

The bridge between the two address types. A device knows the IP it wants but needs a MAC to build the frame, so it broadcasts: "who has 192.168.1.20? tell me your MAC!" The owner replies; the answer is cached in an ARP table.

Shouting a name in a room and waiting for a raised hand.

⏳ TTL (Time To Live)

A countdown in every packet (starts ≈64). Each router subtracts 1; at 0 the packet is destroyed. Without it, a misconfigured network could make packets loop forever.

A self-destruct timer so lost mail doesn't circle the post system for eternity.

🧅 OSI layers (the chips above)

A mental model splitting networking into 7 jobs: 7–5 the app and its data, 4 which program (ports, TCP), 3 which machine in the world (IP, routers), 2 next hop on this cable (MAC, switches), 1 raw electrical/optical signals. Each device only reads up to the layer it needs — that's why switches are fast and routers are smart.

Layers of an onion: each hop peels only as deep as its job requires.
